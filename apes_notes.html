Biological Bases of Behavior

Neurons, brain, nervous system, sensation, sleep

~14–17% of exam

Neuron Anatomy & Firing

  • Dendrites — receive signals from other neurons
  • Axon — carries signal away from cell body
  • Myelin sheath — speeds up signal transmission
  • Axon terminals — release neurotransmitters into synapse
  • Resting potential — neuron is negatively charged inside (−70mV)
  • Action potential — electrical charge travels down axon when threshold is met
  • All-or-nothing law — neuron fires completely or not at all
  • Refractory period — brief recovery; neuron can't fire again immediately

Key Neurotransmitters

  • Dopamine — reward, motivation, movement (low → Parkinson's; high → schizophrenia)
  • Serotonin — mood, sleep, appetite (low → depression)
  • Norepinephrine — arousal, alertness, fight-or-flight
  • GABA — main inhibitory NT; calms neural activity
  • Glutamate — main excitatory NT; learning & memory
  • Acetylcholine (ACh) — muscle movement, memory (low → Alzheimer's)
  • Endorphins — pain relief, pleasure (natural opiates)
Tip: Agonists mimic or enhance NT action. Antagonists block NT receptors.

Brain Structures

  • Medulla — breathing, heart rate (brainstem)
  • Cerebellum — balance, coordination, fine motor
  • Reticular formation — arousal, sleep/wake cycle
  • Thalamus — sensory relay station (except smell)
  • Hypothalamus — hunger, thirst, temp, sex drive, 4 F's
  • Hippocampus — forms new explicit memories
  • Amygdala — fear, aggression, emotional memory
  • Corpus callosum — connects left & right hemispheres
  • Cerebral cortex — higher-order thinking, 4 lobes

4 Lobes of the Cortex

  • Frontal lobe — executive function, personality, motor cortex, Broca's area (speech production)
  • Parietal lobe — somatosensory cortex, spatial processing, touch
  • Occipital lobe — visual processing
  • Temporal lobe — auditory processing, language comprehension, Wernicke's area
Broca's = can't produce speech. Wernicke's = can't understand speech.

Nervous System Divisions

  • CNS — brain + spinal cord
  • PNS — everything outside CNS
  • Somatic NS — voluntary muscle control
  • Autonomic NS — involuntary (heart, glands, organs)
  • Sympathetic — fight-or-flight; speeds up, dilates pupils
  • Parasympathetic — rest-and-digest; slows things down

Sleep Stages

  • Stage 1 — light sleep, hypnagogic hallucinations, theta waves
  • Stage 2 — sleep spindles, K-complexes; harder to wake
  • Stages 3–4 — deep/slow-wave sleep, delta waves; physical restoration
  • REM sleep — rapid eye movement, dreams, memory consolidation, brain very active, body paralyzed
  • REM increases as night goes on; first cycle has least REM
  • REM rebound — more REM after sleep deprivation

Sensation Basics

  • Absolute threshold — minimum stimulus to detect 50% of the time
  • Difference threshold / JND — smallest detectable change between stimuli
  • Weber's Law — JND is a constant proportion of stimulus intensity
  • Signal detection theory — detecting stimuli depends on sensitivity + expectations/motivation
  • Sensory adaptation — reduced sensitivity to constant stimuli
  • Transduction — converting stimuli into neural signals

Endocrine System

  • Pituitary gland — "master gland"; controlled by hypothalamus
  • Adrenal glands — secrete epinephrine & cortisol (stress response)
  • Thyroid — regulates metabolism
  • Hormones — slower than neurotransmitters; travel through bloodstream

Cognition

Memory, intelligence, thinking, language

~17–21% of exam

Memory: Encoding → Storage → Retrieval

Types of Memory

  • Sensory memory — brief, large capacity (iconic = visual; echoic = auditory)
  • Short-term (working) memory — ~7±2 items, ~20 sec without rehearsal
  • Long-term memory — unlimited capacity, long duration
  • Explicit (declarative) — facts (semantic) and events (episodic); requires conscious recall
  • Implicit — skills/habits (procedural), classical conditioning; unconscious

Encoding Strategies

  • Automatic processing — time, space, frequency (no effort)
  • Effortful processing — rehearsal, chunking, mnemonics
  • Spacing effect — distributed practice beats cramming
  • Serial position effect — primacy (first items) + recency (last items) best recalled
  • Levels of processing — deeper semantic encoding = better retention

Forgetting

  • Encoding failure — info never stored properly
  • Proactive interference — old memories interfere with new
  • Retroactive interference — new memories interfere with old
  • Motivated forgetting / repression — Freudian; blocking painful memories
  • Ebbinghaus forgetting curve — most forgetting occurs soon after learning
  • Tip-of-the-tongue — retrieval failure with a feeling of knowing

Memory Biases & Errors

  • Misinformation effect — post-event info alters memories (Loftus)
  • Source amnesia — can't recall where you learned something
  • Schemas — mental frameworks that distort memory to fit expectations
  • Flashbulb memories — vivid, emotional memories (not necessarily more accurate)
  • Déjà vu — feeling of having experienced something before

Intelligence Theories

  • Spearman's g — general intelligence factor underlying all cognitive abilities
  • Gardner's Multiple Intelligences — 8+ types (linguistic, logical, musical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic)
  • Sternberg's Triarchic — Analytical, Creative, Practical intelligence
  • Fluid intelligence — reasoning, novel problem-solving (peaks early, declines with age)
  • Crystallized intelligence — accumulated knowledge (increases with age)

Thinking & Problem Solving

  • Algorithm — step-by-step rule that guarantees solution
  • Heuristic — mental shortcut; fast but error-prone
  • Availability heuristic — judge probability by how easily examples come to mind
  • Representativeness heuristic — judge by resemblance to prototype
  • Confirmation bias — seeking info that confirms existing beliefs
  • Fixation — inability to see problem from new angle
  • Framing effect — how a question is worded affects decision

Language

  • Phoneme — smallest unit of sound
  • Morpheme — smallest unit of meaning
  • Linguistic relativity (Whorf) — language shapes thought
  • Critical period — language best acquired early in life (Chomsky's LAD)
  • Babbling → one-word → two-word → telegraphic speech → full language

IQ & Testing Concepts

  • Reliability — consistent results
  • Validity — measures what it claims to measure
  • Standardization — uniform procedure + norms
  • Flynn effect — IQ scores have risen over generations
  • Stereotype threat — awareness of negative stereotypes impairs performance
  • Normal distribution — IQ mean = 100, SD = 15

Development and Learning

Classical conditioning, operant conditioning, observational, lifespan

~20–24% of exam

Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

Before Conditioning
UCS → UCR
Food → Salivate (natural)
Conditioning Phase
NS + UCS → UCR
Bell + Food → Salivate
After Conditioning
CS → CR
Bell alone → Salivate
Key Processes
Extinction — CS repeatedly without UCS → CR weakens
Spontaneous recovery — extinguished CR returns after rest
Generalization — similar stimuli trigger CR
Discrimination — only respond to specific CS

Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

TypeAdd (+) or Remove (−)Effect on BehaviorExample
Positive ReinforcementAdd desirable stimulusIncreases behaviorTreat for sitting
Negative ReinforcementRemove unpleasant stimulusIncreases behaviorTaking painkillers removes pain
Positive PunishmentAdd unpleasant stimulusDecreases behaviorSpanking, speeding ticket
Negative PunishmentRemove desirable stimulusDecreases behaviorTaking away phone
Schedules of Reinforcement: Fixed ratio = most responses per reward (vending), Variable ratio = most resistant to extinction (gambling/slot machines), Fixed interval = scallop pattern, Variable interval = steady rate

Observational Learning (Bandura)

  • Modeling — learning by watching others
  • Bobo doll experiment — kids imitated aggressive adult model
  • Requires: Attention → Retention → Reproduction → Motivation
  • Self-efficacy — belief in one's ability to succeed
  • Vicarious reinforcement — more likely to imitate rewarded models

Cognitive Learning

  • Latent learning — learning occurs without immediate reinforcement (Tolman's rats + cognitive maps)
  • Insight learning — sudden "aha!" moment (Köhler's chimps)
  • Learned helplessness — believing one has no control leads to passivity (Seligman)
  • Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation — internal enjoyment vs. external reward

Piaget's Stages (Cognitive)

  • Sensorimotor (0–2) — object permanence; world through senses/action
  • Preoperational (2–7) — symbolic thinking, egocentrism, no conservation
  • Concrete Operational (7–11) — conservation, logical thinking about concrete objects
  • Formal Operational (12+) — abstract reasoning, hypothetical thinking
  • Assimilation — fitting new info into existing schemas
  • Accommodation — changing schemas to fit new info

Social Development

  • Harlow's monkeys — contact comfort > food; attachment to cloth mother
  • Ainsworth's Strange Situation — secure, anxious-ambivalent, avoidant attachment
  • Secure attachment — explores freely, distressed when parent leaves, comforted on return
  • Vygotsky — Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) — what child can do with guidance; scaffolding
  • Erikson's stages — 8 psychosocial crises across lifespan (e.g., trust vs. mistrust, identity vs. role confusion)

Adolescent & Adult Development

  • Puberty — physical maturation; begins earlier for girls
  • Kohlberg's moral development — Preconventional → Conventional → Postconventional
  • Identity vs. role confusion — Erikson's adolescent stage
  • Emerging adulthood — Arnett's concept; age 18–25 period of identity exploration
  • Menopause — end of female reproductive period; associated changes

Social Psychology & Personality

Conformity, attribution, group behavior, personality theories

~20–24% of exam

Attribution Theory

  • Attribution — explaining behavior by identifying causes
  • Dispositional (internal) — blame/credit person's character
  • Situational (external) — blame/credit circumstances
  • Fundamental attribution error (FAE) — overemphasize dispositional, underestimate situational causes for others' behavior
  • Self-serving bias — attribute own successes to self, failures to situation
  • Actor-observer effect — attribute own behavior situationally, others' dispositionally

Attitudes & Persuasion

  • Cognitive dissonance — tension when actions conflict with beliefs; we change beliefs to reduce it (Festinger)
  • Foot-in-the-door — small request first, then larger (compliance)
  • Door-in-the-face — large request first (refused), then smaller target request
  • Central route persuasion — logic and evidence; lasting attitude change
  • Peripheral route — superficial cues (attractiveness, emotion); temporary change

Conformity & Obedience

  • Asch (conformity) — people give wrong answers to match group; ~75% conformed at least once
  • Milgram (obedience) — ~65% of participants administered maximum "shock" when ordered by authority
  • Normative influence — conforming to fit in / be liked
  • Informational influence — conforming because others seem to know better
  • Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Study — people conform to assigned roles (ethics: deception, harm)

Group Behavior

  • Social facilitation — presence of others improves simple tasks, impairs complex ones
  • Social loafing — less effort in groups
  • Deindividuation — loss of self-awareness in groups → impulsive behavior
  • Groupthink — desire for harmony suppresses dissent; poor decisions
  • Group polarization — group discussion strengthens members' existing opinions
  • Bystander effect — less likely to help when others are present (diffusion of responsibility)

Prejudice & Discrimination

  • Stereotype — generalized belief about a group
  • Prejudice — unjustified attitude (affective)
  • Discrimination — unjustified action based on prejudice
  • In-group vs. out-group — favoritism for own group; hostility toward others
  • Scapegoating — blaming an out-group for one's problems
  • Contact hypothesis — equal-status contact reduces prejudice
  • Just-world phenomenon — belief that people get what they deserve

Psychodynamic Personality (Freud)

  • Id — pleasure principle, unconscious drives
  • Ego — reality principle, mediates id & superego
  • Superego — morality, internalized rules
  • Defense mechanisms — repression, projection, rationalization, displacement, regression, reaction formation, sublimation
  • Psychosexual stages: oral → anal → phallic (Oedipus/Electra) → latency → genital

Humanistic Personality (Maslow, Rogers)

  • Maslow's hierarchy — physiological → safety → love/belonging → esteem → self-actualization
  • Self-actualization — fulfilling one's full potential
  • Carl Rogers — unconditional positive regard, self-concept, congruence
  • Congruence — match between ideal self and actual self = healthy personality

Trait Theories & Social-Cognitive

  • Big Five (OCEAN) — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism
  • Bandura's social-cognitive — behavior influenced by cognition, environment, and behavior (reciprocal determinism)
  • Locus of control — internal (control own fate) vs. external (luck/fate controls)
  • Projective tests — Rorschach (inkblots), TAT; reveal unconscious (low reliability)
  • Objective tests — MMPI; standardized self-report

Aggression & Prosocial Behavior

  • Frustration-aggression hypothesis — frustrated goals trigger aggression
  • Catharsis — venting reduces aggression (largely unsupported by research)
  • Altruism — selfless helping behavior
  • Reciprocity norm — feel obligated to help those who've helped us
  • Social exchange theory — weigh costs/benefits of helping

Mental and Physical Health

Psychological disorders, treatment, stress, positive psychology

~17–21% of exam

Classifying Disorders: The DSM-5

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders defines criteria for diagnosing psychological disorders. The biopsychosocial model holds that disorders arise from biological (genetics, neurotransmitters), psychological (learning, cognition), and social (culture, stress, relationships) factors.

Criteria for a disorder: Distress, Dysfunction, Deviance (unusual), and Danger — the "4 D's"

Anxiety Disorders

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) — chronic, uncontrollable worry about many things
  • Panic Disorder — recurring unexpected panic attacks; fear of having them
  • Phobia — intense irrational fear of specific object/situation
  • Social Anxiety Disorder — fear of social situations, being judged
  • OCD — obsessions (intrusive thoughts) + compulsions (repetitive behaviors to reduce anxiety)
  • PTSD — flashbacks, hyperarousal, avoidance after traumatic event

Mood Disorders

  • Major Depressive Disorder — persistent low mood, hopelessness, loss of interest ≥2 weeks
  • Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia) — chronic mild depression ≥2 years
  • Bipolar I — full manic episodes + depressive episodes
  • Bipolar II — hypomanic episodes + severe depression
  • Mania — elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, impulsivity, grandiosity
  • Linked to low serotonin/norepinephrine (depression), dopamine dysregulation (bipolar)

Schizophrenia Spectrum

  • Positive symptoms — added behaviors: hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech
  • Negative symptoms — removed behaviors: flat affect, alogia (reduced speech), avolition (no motivation)
  • Hallucinations — perceiving without stimulus (most often auditory)
  • Delusions — fixed false beliefs (persecution, grandeur)
  • Dopamine hypothesis — excess dopamine activity linked to positive symptoms
  • Enlarged ventricles and reduced prefrontal activity noted in brain scans

Dissociative & Personality Disorders

  • Dissociative Identity Disorder — two or more distinct personality states
  • Dissociative amnesia — inability to recall personal info due to psychological trauma
  • Antisocial Personality Disorder — disregard for others' rights, no remorse (sociopathy)
  • Borderline Personality Disorder — unstable identity, mood, and relationships; impulsivity
  • Narcissistic Personality Disorder — grandiosity, lack of empathy

Neurodevelopmental Disorders

  • ADHD — inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity; treated with stimulants
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder — social/communication difficulties, restricted behaviors/interests
  • Intellectual Disability — significant limits in intellectual functioning + adaptive behavior
  • Specific Learning Disorder — dyslexia (reading), dyscalculia (math)

Biomedical Treatments

  • Antidepressants (SSRIs) — increase serotonin (e.g., Prozac, Zoloft); used for depression, anxiety, OCD
  • Antipsychotics — block dopamine; treat schizophrenia
  • Anti-anxiety (benzodiazepines) — enhance GABA; short-term (habit-forming)
  • Mood stabilizers — lithium for bipolar disorder
  • ECT (Electroconvulsive therapy) — last resort for severe depression; effective but causes memory loss
  • Psychosurgery — lobotomy (historical); deep brain stimulation (modern)

Psychological Therapies

  • Psychoanalysis — free association, dream analysis, transference; uncover unconscious conflicts
  • Humanistic (person-centered) — Rogers; unconditional positive regard, empathy, genuineness
  • Cognitive therapy (Beck) — challenge negative automatic thoughts and cognitive distortions
  • CBT — most evidence-based; combines cognitive + behavioral techniques
  • Behavior therapies — systematic desensitization (phobias), token economy, aversion therapy
  • EMDR — eye movement desensitization for PTSD

Stress & Health

  • General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye) — Alarm → Resistance → Exhaustion
  • HPA axis — hypothalamus → pituitary → adrenal glands → cortisol release
  • Type A personality — competitive, impatient, hostile; higher heart disease risk
  • Problem-focused coping — address the stressor directly
  • Emotion-focused coping — manage emotional response to stressor
  • Social support — strong predictor of health outcomes
  • Positive psychology (Seligman) — focus on strengths, well-being, meaning (PERMA model)

FRQ Strategy — How to Get Full Points

Format Rules

  • • Each part (A, B, C...) = separate paragraph
  • • Define the term first, then apply to the scenario
  • • Be specific — name the concept, explain how it applies
  • • Don't just list; write in complete sentences
  • • Underline or bold key terms (helps graders)
  • • There are 2 FRQs — 25 min each is a good pace

Common FRQ Topics

  • • Classical vs. operant conditioning scenarios
  • • Research methods (IV, DV, confounds, bias)
  • • Applying a disorder to a case study
  • • Neurotransmitter + drug effects
  • • Explaining behavior using multiple units
  • • Piaget / Erikson development scenarios
